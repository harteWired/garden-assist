# Groundhog Fencing Project — Detailed Build Guide

## Project Overview

Build a single welded-wire enclosure around both raised beds, with a buried anti-dig apron and wobble-top to defeat climbers. This is your most critical garden infrastructure — nothing else matters without it.

**Timeline:** Complete by mid-April, before outdoor planting begins.
**Difficulty:** Moderate. The trench is the hard part.
**Estimated build time:** 1-2 weekends (Day 1: trench + posts, Day 2: wire + gate)

---

## Enclosure Dimensions

Assuming your two 4×12 beds are side-by-side with ~3-4 feet between them, oriented roughly east-west:

**Interior footprint:** ~16' wide × 16' long (12' bed length + 2' clearance each end, 4'+4' beds + 4' gap between + 2' clearance each side)

Adjust based on actual spacing. You want **minimum 18-24 inches** of clearance between the fence and the bed edges on all sides — enough to walk, kneel, and wheelbarrow through.

**Perimeter:** ~64 linear feet
**With gate and overlap allowance:** Buy ~80 feet of fencing material

## Digging the Trench — The Hard Part

### Trench Spec
- **Depth:** 12 inches
- **Width:** 6-8 inches (just wide enough for the L-apron)
- **Length:** Full perimeter (~64 feet)
- **Shape:** Straight-sided, doesn't need to be pretty

### Tool Options for the Trench

**Option A: Rent a Trencher (RECOMMENDED)**
- A walk-behind trencher (like a Ditch Witch or similar) will cut a 12" deep × 4-6" wide trench in minutes instead of hours.
- **Rental cost:** ~$150-200/day from Home Depot, Sunbelt Rentals, or a local equipment rental.
- **Availability:** Call ahead — spring is busy season. Reserve for a Saturday.
- **Pros:** Fast (under an hour for 64 feet), consistent depth, saves your back.
- **Cons:** Requires transport (trailer or truck), some learning curve, may struggle in very rocky soil (Warren County NJ can have shale — probe first).
- **Transport:** Most rental places offer delivery for an extra fee, or you can tow on a utility trailer. The smaller walk-behind models fit in a truck bed.

**Option B: Rent a Mini Excavator with Trenching Bucket**
- Overkill for this job, but if you have other landscaping work to do, a mini excavator rental (~$250-350/day) does everything.
- Only worth it if you're combining projects.

**Option C: Manual Digging (Buy Tools)**
- If soil is loose/loamy, manual is doable in a long day.
- If you hit clay, shale, or lots of roots from those evergreens, you'll curse every foot.

**Manual digging tool kit:**

| Tool | Purpose | Buy Price |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Trenching shovel (narrow blade) | Primary digging tool — 4" wide blade designed for narrow trenches | $30-40 |
| Mattock or pickaxe | Breaking through roots and rocky soil | $30-40 |
| Digging bar / pry bar | Loosening rocks, prying | $25-35 |
| Garden spade | Cleanup and squaring trench walls | (own) |

**My recommendation:** Rent the walk-behind trencher if you can get one. 64 feet of manual trenching in potentially rocky NJ soil is a full day of hard labor. The $150-200 rental pays for itself in time and back pain. Call your local Home Depot tool rental or search for equipment rental in Warren County.

### Pre-Dig Checklist

1. **Call 811 (NJ One Call) at least 3 business days before digging.** Free service — they'll mark buried utilities. Non-negotiable. Even for a 12" trench, you don't want to hit a gas line.
2. **Probe the soil** along your planned perimeter with a piece of rebar or a metal rod. Push it down 12". If you hit rock at 6", you'll know to adjust your plan (shallower trench + longer L-apron laid on the surface and covered with soil).
3. **Mark your perimeter** with stakes and string. Keep 18-24" out from the bed edges.
4. **Check for tree roots** from the evergreens. Their roots spread wide. You may hit significant roots on the west side. Cut cleanly with loppers or a reciprocating saw — don't tear them.

### Digging Process (Manual Method)

1. Cut along both edges of the trench line with a garden spade to define the edges
2. Use the trenching shovel to remove soil in 6" lifts — don't try to go full depth in one pass
3. Pile dirt on the OUTSIDE of the fence line (you'll backfill from this pile later)
4. For roots: cut cleanly, don't yank. Ripping roots damages the tree.
5. For rocks: pry with the digging bar, don't fight rocks bigger than a basketball — go around them
6. Target 12" depth. If you can only get 8-10" in spots due to rock, compensate with a longer horizontal apron (extend it 12-18" outward instead of 6")

## Build Sequence

### Step 1: Layout & Trench (Day 1 Morning)

1. Stake the four corners of your enclosure
2. Run string between stakes — this is your fence line
3. Measure and confirm you have 18-24" clearance to beds on all sides
4. Mark gate location (recommend the side closest to your house / garden hose)
5. Dig the trench along the full perimeter

### Step 2: Set Posts (Day 1 Afternoon)

1. Place T-posts every 5-6 feet around the perimeter
2. Corner posts go RIGHT at the corners
3. Gate posts get two T-posts side by side (or use 4×4 wood posts set in concrete for the gate — more stable for hanging a gate)
4. Drive T-posts with the post driver: place post, driver over top, lift and slam. Posts should be ~18-24" in the ground with the rest above grade
5. Face the flat side of T-posts (the side with the nubs/clips) INWARD — this is where the wire attaches

### Step 3: Attach Fencing (Day 2 Morning)

1. Unroll welded wire along the perimeter, OUTSIDE the trench
2. Starting at one corner post, attach wire to the post using tie wire or heavy zip ties every 8-12 inches vertically
3. **Bottom of the fence goes INTO the trench:** Bend the bottom 12" of fencing outward at a 90° angle to form the L-apron. This bent portion lies flat in the bottom of the trench, pointing AWAY from the enclosure.
4. If using separate hardware cloth for the apron: lay it flat in the trench bottom, overlapping with the vertical fencing by 4-6". Tie them together with wire.
5. Pull fencing taut between posts. Attach to each post as you go.
6. At corners, cut the wire, wrap it around the corner post, and start the next run. Or bend the wire around the corner (use a board as a bending jig).

### Step 4: The Wobble Top (Day 2)

Groundhogs can climb rigid fencing. The wobble top defeats them:

1. Leave the TOP 12" of your 4' fencing **unattached** to posts or any support rail
2. Bend this 12" outward at roughly 45° — it should flop loosely when pushed
3. A groundhog climbing the fence hits this wobbly overhang and can't get leverage to climb over
4. If your fencing is very rigid, use separate chicken wire attached at the top of the welded wire, extending outward. This is floppy enough to work.

### Step 5: Backfill the Trench

1. Push the piled dirt back into the trench, covering the buried apron
2. Tamp firmly with your foot or a tamper
3. The buried apron should be completely hidden under soil
4. Over time, grass/weeds will grow over it, locking it in place

### Step 6: Build and Hang the Gate

1. Measure your gate opening (aim for 36-40" wide — enough for a wheelbarrow)
2. Build a rectangular frame from 2×4 lumber: two vertical stiles + two horizontal rails + one diagonal brace (from bottom hinge side to top latch side — prevents sagging)
3. Staple welded wire across the frame
4. Hang with strap hinges on one gate post
5. Install a barrel bolt or hook latch on the other side
6. **Critical: no gap under the gate.** Add a wire mesh threshold or ensure the gate frame sits flush with the ground. A 3" gap is an open invitation.

## Maintenance

- Inspect the fence monthly during growing season, especially the base
- Look for dig marks at the fence line — this means an animal is testing your defenses
- If you see digging, add more soil over the apron in that spot and consider extending the apron with additional mesh
- Replace any rusted or damaged wire sections before they become weak points
- Snow and ice can deform welded wire — check the fence after winter

## Alternative: Simpler Approach for Raised Beds

Since your beds are raised 12" off the ground, you have a shortcut option:

Instead of fencing a large perimeter, you can attach hardware cloth directly to the SIDES of each raised bed, extending 3-4 feet upward. This turns each bed into its own cage. Advantages: less material, no trench needed (the bed itself is the barrier). Disadvantages: harder to work inside, annoying to access plants, looks like a prison garden.

The perimeter fence is the better long-term solution. But if you need something FAST while you build the real fence, stapling hardware cloth to the bed sides as a temporary barrier will buy you a few weeks.
