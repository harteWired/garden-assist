## Soil Block Mix Recipe

Your 2-year aged compost is perfect for this. Sift it through 1/4" hardware cloth first — you don't want chunks. Here's a proven mix:

**For ~2" blocks (what you want for most transplants):**
- 4 parts coconut coir (rehydrated) or peat moss
- 1 part perlite
- 1 part sifted compost (your aged stuff)
- Per gallon of mix: 1/4 cup greensand + 1/4 cup rock phosphate (or substitute worm castings for both if easier to source)

If using peat moss, add 1/4 cup dolomite lime per gallon to balance pH. If using coir, skip the lime.

**Critical technique tips for your first time:**
- Mix WETTER than you think. When you squeeze a handful, water should drip freely — like a wrung-out sponge that's still pretty wet.
- Press the blocker firmly into the mix, twist slightly, then press onto your tray with authority. Hesitation = crumbly blocks.
- Bottom water only. Set blocks in a tray with no holes, add water to the tray, let blocks wick it up. Top watering destroys blocks.
- Under grow lights: keep lights 2-4" above blocks/seedlings. Run 14-16 hours/day. Weak light = leggy seedlings = wasted effort.
- Blocks should stay moist but not sitting in standing water. Check daily.

## Tomato Variety Picks for Part Shade

You're getting ~5-6 hours of sun with evergreen shade, so you need varieties that mature fast and/or tolerate lower light. Go with cherry/small-fruited types as your primary and try one or two larger ones as experiments.

**Primary picks (best chance of success):**
- **Sungold** — cherry tomato, incredibly prolific, fast ripener, widely considered the best-tasting cherry ever grown. This is your #1 pick.
- **Black Cherry** — slightly more complex flavor, very high yields, documented shade tolerance
- **Juliet** — grape/plum hybrid, prolific, disease resistant, great for snacking and cooking

**Worth trying (may underperform but will likely produce something):**
- **Black Krim** — heirloom, documented at producing in 4-5 hours of sun. Rich, smoky flavor. Lower yield but exceptional taste.
- **Stupice** — Czech heirloom, one of the earliest tomatoes to ripen (60 days), cold tolerant, does well with less sun
- **Siberia** — matures in ~55 days, sets fruit in cool temps down to 38°F, designed for short/tough seasons

**Grow strategy:** Plant tomatoes on the SOUTH-facing long edge of one bed to maximize their sun exposure. Stake or cage them. Prune aggressively to single or double leaders — in low light, you want the plant putting energy into ripening fewer fruits rather than growing a jungle. Remove suckers religiously.

```
├─────────────────────┴────────────┴───────────────┤

│                │               │ or Kohlrabi     │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤

│  THEN: scallions + carrots after peas finish July │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
SOUTH EDGE (most sun)
```

**Key layout logic:**
- Tallest crops (tomatoes, trellised peas/cucumbers) go on the north side of their respective beds so they don't shade shorter crops
- Exception: peas go on the south edge of Bed 2 because they're an early cool-season crop that will be DONE by July, then you replant that row with warm-season stuff
- Lettuce, spinach, arugula on the shadiest edges — they actually prefer it
- Brassicas in the middle — they tolerate shade well and bolt slower with less direct sun

## Groundhog Defense — Build Specification

This is non-negotiable. Without it, you're growing an all-you-can-eat buffet for wildlife.

**Materials (for BOTH beds, ~80 linear feet total plus gates):**
- 2x4" welded wire fencing, 4' tall — 1 roll of 50' should cover it if you fence each bed individually, or buy 100' to be safe and have material for gates
- 4-6 metal T-posts per bed (every 4-5 feet around perimeter)
- Heavy-gauge zip ties or galvanized wire for attaching mesh to posts
- 1/2" hardware cloth, 24" wide, ~80 linear feet — this is your buried anti-dig barrier (optional upgrade from using the welded wire itself underground)
- Gate hardware or plan for step-over access

**Build spec:**
1. Trench around each bed perimeter, 12" deep, about 6" out from the bed wall
2. Bend 12" of the bottom of your fencing outward in an L-shape (or use separate hardware cloth as an apron). The L goes AWAY from the bed — when a groundhog digs at the fence line, it hits more fence instead of open ground
3. Set T-posts, hang 4' welded wire. You want 3.5-4' above grade
4. At the TOP, leave the last 12" of fencing unattached to any post/rail so it flops outward. This wobbly overhang defeats climbers. Alternatively, bend the top 12" outward at a 45° angle
5. Backfill the trench, tamp soil over the buried apron
6. Build a simple gate or step-over section. A wooden frame with welded wire stapled to it, hung on strap hinges, works fine. Make sure the gate bottom has no gap — add a mesh threshold

**Timeline:** Get this done by mid-April at the latest. Order materials now. The groundhogs will be active by April.

**Budget approach:** If you want to save money, you can fence both beds together as one unit rather than individually — less fencing, one gate. Just leave working room between the beds and the fence (18-24" minimum).

## Soil Prep for Raised Beds

Your 2-year compost is excellent. Here's how to prep the beds:

1. **Top-dress with 2-3" of your aged compost** and work it into the top 6" of existing bed soil
2. **Add amendments:** Mix in per bed (4x12 = 48 sq ft):
   - 5-10 lbs of granular organic all-purpose fertilizer (something like Espoma Garden-Tone or similar, 3-4-4 or close)
   - 2-3 lbs of greensand (slow-release potassium + minerals)
   - Optional: 1 lb of azomite or rock dust for trace minerals
3. **If soil feels heavy/compacted**, work in perlite or coarse vermiculite to improve drainage
4. **Check pH** if you can — most veggies want 6.0-7.0. Your aged compost should be close. A simple soil test kit from a garden center costs $15 and tells you pH + NPK levels. Worth doing once.
5. **Do NOT add fresh/hot manure or un-aged compost.** Your 2-year stuff is ready to go.

Do this 2-3 weeks before your first outdoor planting (so late March / early April).

## Season-Long Care Notes for Part Shade

- **Water carefully.** Shade = slower evaporation = higher risk of overwatering and fungal disease. Let the top inch dry between waterings. Avoid wetting foliage, especially on tomatoes.
- **Airflow is critical.** Don't overcrowd. Space plants at or slightly wider than recommended distances. Prune lower tomato leaves. This reduces disease pressure, which is higher in shady, moist conditions.
- **Fertilize moderately.** With less sun, plants grow slower and need less food. Don't push nitrogen-heavy fertilizer on fruiting crops — you'll get big plants with few fruits. Side-dress tomatoes with compost or balanced fertilizer once they start setting fruit.
- **Expect longer maturity times.** Everything takes longer in part shade. A tomato that says "75 days" on the packet might take 90+ for you. Plan accordingly.
- **Slug/snail watch.** Shady, moist beds are slug paradise. Beer traps, diatomaceous earth, or iron phosphate bait (Sluggo) — have a plan.
- **Harvest greens aggressively.** Cut-and-come-again lettuce and spinach do better when you harvest outer leaves frequently. This keeps them producing instead of bolting.
